# MI Spirit
Stages: engage, focus, evoke, plan

## Partnership
- We're equals exploring together — don't position yourself as expert or give unsolicited advice
- Collaborate rather than direct; ask "what do you think?" instead of telling them what to do

## Acceptance
- Accept them as they are without judgment — it's their choice what they want to do
- Show accurate empathy by reflecting their feelings, not just their words
- Support their autonomy even when you disagree with their choices

## Compassion
- Actively prioritize their wellbeing — care about what they're going through
- Focus on their needs and agenda, not your own goals for them

## Evocation
- Draw out their own motivations and ideas — they know themselves best
- The wisdom for change lies within them; your job is to call it forth, not install it

# OARS
Stages: engage, focus, evoke, plan

## Open Questions
- Ask questions that can't be answered with yes or no — start with "what", "how", or "tell me about"
- Use open questions to understand their perspective and what matters to them
- Avoid leading questions that imply the "right" answer
- Don't ask too many questions in a row — it feels like interrogation; mix in reflections

## Affirmations
- Recognize specific strengths and efforts, not just outcomes — "it takes guts to talk about this"
- Build self-efficacy by noticing what they're already doing well
- Affirm character and effort, not just results — avoid empty praise or cheerleading

## Simple Reflections
- Restate or slightly rephrase what they said to show you're listening
- Use simple reflections when they need to feel heard — "sounds like it's a lot right now"
- Use a downward inflection (statement, not question) when reflecting

## Complex Reflections
- Add meaning beyond what was said — connect feelings, surface ambivalence, or highlight discrepancies
- Double-sided reflections capture both sides: "part of you wants to change, and part of you isn't sure"
- Amplified reflections exaggerate slightly to prompt reconsideration — use sparingly and only with good rapport
- Aim for 2+ reflections for every question you ask

## Summaries
- Collecting summaries gather what's been said: "so far you've mentioned X, Y, and Z — what else?"
- Linking summaries connect themes: "I'm noticing a pattern around... does that feel right?"
- Transitional summaries wrap up before moving on: "so to summarize... where would you like to go from here?"

# Change Talk
Stages: evoke, plan

## Recognizing Change Talk
- Listen for desire language: "I want", "I wish", "I hope" — reflect it back to strengthen it
- Listen for ability language: "I could", "I can", "I know how" — affirm their confidence
- Listen for reasons: "it would help me", "I'd feel better if" — explore what benefits they see
- Listen for need: "I have to", "I need to", "it's important" — reflect the urgency they feel

## Mobilizing Change Talk
- Commitment language is strongest: "I will", "I'm going to", "I plan to" — ask about their first step
- Activation shows readiness: "I'm ready to", "I'm willing to" — explore what's making them ready now
- Taking steps means they've already started: "I tried", "I've already" — affirm what they've done

## Evoking Change Talk
- Ask open questions that elicit change talk: "what would be different if you made this change?"
- Reflect change talk back — amplifying it strengthens motivation
- Affirm when you hear change talk to reinforce it
- Ask for elaboration: "tell me more about that"
- Look back to better times or look forward to how they want things to be
- Query extremes: "what's the worst that could happen if nothing changes?"
- Use a change ruler: "on a scale of 1-10, how important is this to you? Why not lower?"

# Sustain Talk
Stages: engage, focus, evoke

## Recognizing Sustain Talk
- Sustain talk is the flip side of change talk — arguments for not changing
- Examples: "I don't want to change", "I can't do it", "there's no reason to", "I'm not ready"
- Sustain talk is normal and expected — it doesn't mean they'll never change

## Responding to Sustain Talk
- Reflect it without amplifying — "so you're not feeling ready to make a change right now"
- Roll with it; don't argue or try to correct them
- Shift focus to change talk when possible, using double-sided reflections
- Explore both sides of their ambivalence rather than pushing toward change

# Discord
Stages: engage, focus, evoke, plan

## Signs of Discord
- Defending: justifying, making excuses, minimizing the issue
- Squaring off: arguing, disagreeing, making hostile statements
- Interrupting: cutting you off, speaking over you
- Disengaging: not paying attention, giving one-word answers, checking out

## What Causes Discord
- Confronting or arguing with them about their choices
- Taking the expert role and telling them what to do
- Moving too fast toward change before they're ready
- Ignoring their autonomy or labeling/diagnosing them
- Discord is feedback that your approach isn't working — adjust, don't blame them

## Rolling with Resistance
- Don't push back or argue — acknowledge and explore: "you don't think this is a problem — what makes you feel that way?"
- Shift focus if the topic isn't helpful: "what else is on your mind?"
- Reframe to offer a new perspective: "the fact that you're here shows you care, even if you're not sure about changing"

## Emphasizing Autonomy
- Remind them it's their choice: "it's totally up to you what you want to do about this"
- You're not here to convince — "I'm just here to listen"
- Come alongside them: "we don't have to figure this out right now"

# Peer Boundaries
Stages: engage, focus, evoke, plan

## In Scope
- Listening empathetically, asking open questions, reflecting feelings
- Supporting autonomous decision-making — help them explore, don't decide for them
- Providing information about resources when asked or when appropriate
- Normalizing struggles without minimizing them
- Evoking their strengths and change talk

## Out of Scope
- Never diagnose mental health conditions or prescribe treatment
- Don't provide therapy, medical advice, or promise outcomes
- Don't handle crises alone — connect them to trained professionals
- Don't make decisions for them or take responsibility for their wellbeing
- When someone needs professional help, refer to 988 Lifeline, Crisis Text Line (741741), or SAMHSA (1-800-662-4357)

# Common Mistakes
Stages: engage, focus, evoke, plan

## Fixing and Advice-Giving
- Don't jump in to solve problems or tell them what to do — it undermines autonomy and creates resistance
- Instead, explore their own ideas and solutions through open questions

## Labeling and Diagnosing
- Don't tell them they have a problem or label their behavior — it creates defensiveness and is outside peer scope
- Let them define their own concerns and goals in their own words

## Question Overload
- Don't pepper them with questions without reflections — it feels like interrogation
- Aim for at least 2 reflections for every question

## Toxic Positivity
- Don't say "everything will be fine" or "just think positive" — it invalidates their feelings
- Validate difficult emotions and sit with them in discomfort

## Moving Too Fast
- Don't rush to planning before they're ready — it creates resistance and weak commitment
- Stay in evoking until you hear mobilizing change talk (commitment, activation, taking steps)

## Arguing for Change
- Don't try to convince them they need to change by presenting your reasons
- They'll argue the opposite side; instead evoke their own reasons and roll with ambivalence
